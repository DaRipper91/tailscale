# Gemini Skill Manual: lateral-thinking
## Format: 8-5-4 Standard

### Chapter 1: Introduction to Lateral Reasoning
**1.1 Objective**
Break out of traditional, linear thought patterns to find innovative and non-obvious solutions.

**1.2 What is Lateral Thinking?**
A method for problem-solving by approaching problems from new and unexpected angles.

**1.3 The "Brain" of the Skill**
Think like a mix of an inventor, a philosopher, and a high-level systems architect.

**1.4 First-Principles Thinking**
Strip a problem down to its fundamental truths and rebuild from the ground up.

**1.5 Pro-Tip**
Use this skill when you've hit a "wall" or when standard solutions are too expensive, slow, or complex.

---

### Chapter 2: The Provocation Technique
**2.1 Objective**
Use "Impossible" or "Absurd" statements to trigger new ideas.

**2.2 Creating a "Po" (Provocation)**
Make a deliberately false or extreme statement about the problem.
```bash
# Example:
"The car has no wheels." -> Leads to ideas about hovercraft or rails.
```

**2.3 Stepping Stones**
Use the provocation as a bridge to a practical, realistic solution.

**2.4 Movement over Judgment**
Suspend criticism during the early phase to allow "crazy" ideas to flourish.

**2.5 Expected Outcome**
A list of radical alternatives that challenge the status quo of your project.

---

### Chapter 3: Cross-Domain Analogies
**3.1 Objective**
Apply solutions from unrelated fields (e.g., biology, music, architecture) to your problem.

**3.2 Finding the Analogy**
Identify the core "verb" of your problem (e.g., "connecting," "filtering," "shielding").

**3.3 Mapping Solutions**
How does a beehive handle "filtering"? How does a symphony handle "coordination"?

**3.4 Translation to Project**
Adapt the abstract analogy into a concrete technical implementation.

**3.5 Pro-Tip**
Ask the agent: "How would a [Botanist/Pilot/Painter] solve this software bug?"

---

### Chapter 4: Challenging Assumptions
**4.1 Objective**
Identify and break the "hidden rules" that are limiting your options.

**4.2 Listing Assumptions**
Write down everything you believe is "required" or "true" about the task.

**4.3 The "Why?" Audit**
For every assumption, ask "Why?" and "What if this wasn't true?"

**4.4 Breaking the Constraint**
Imagine a world where that constraint doesn't exist. How would the solution change?

**4.5 Expected Outcome**
A "simplified" version of the problem that is often much easier to solve.

---

### Chapter 5: Random Entry & Stimulation
**5.1 Objective**
Use random inputs (words, images, concepts) to jump-start the creative process.

**5.2 The Random Word Generator**
Pick a word that has nothing to do with your task.

**5.3 Forced Connection**
Force a logical link between that random word and your current problem.

**5.4 Idea Synthesis**
The resulting "weird" connection often reveals a hidden opportunity or new feature.

**5.5 Pro-Tip**
If you're stuck on a UI design, pick a random object from your desk and use its shape for inspiration.

---

### Chapter 6: Reframing the Problem
**6.1 Objective**
Change how the problem is stated to open up new solution spaces.

**6.2 The "Inverse" Problem**
Instead of "How do I get more users?", ask "How could I make users hate my product?"

**6.3 Perspective Shifting**
How does a 5-year-old see this? How does a CEO see this? How does the server see this?

**6.4 Expanding the Boundary**
Is the problem actually with the code, or is it a problem with the user's expectations?

**6.5 Expected Outcome**
A new problem statement that points directly toward a more effective solution.

---

### Chapter 7: Systems & Synergy
**7.1 Objective**
Understand how individual lateral ideas interact within the larger system.

**7.2 Identifying Feedback Loops**
Does a lateral solution create a positive or negative loop elsewhere?

**7.3 Emergent Properties**
Can multiple "small" lateral changes combine into a massive systemic improvement?

**7.4 Risk Mitigation**
Audit "crazy" ideas for unintended consequences before implementation.

**7.5 Pro-Tip**
Use lateral thinking during the `conductor/tech-stack.md` phase to avoid industry-standard "traps".

---

### Chapter 8: Workflow Reference & FAQ
**8.1 Objective**
Quick lookup for lateral thinking triggers.

**8.2 Core Methods**
*   Provocation, Analogy, Assumption Breaking, Random Entry, Reframing.

**8.3 FAQ: Is this for coding?**
Yes. It is best for architectural decisions, debugging "impossible" bugs, and UI/UX innovation.

**8.4 FAQ: When should I NOT use it?**
When you need a quick, standard fix for a known problem. Don't reinvent the wheel if it's already rolling.

**8.5 Final Pro-Tip**
Lateral thinking is a muscle. The more you use it, the easier it becomes to see the "shortcuts" others miss.
