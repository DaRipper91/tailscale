# Gemini Skill Manual: critical-analysis
## Format: 8-5-4 Standard

### Chapter 1: Introduction to Logical Auditing
**1.1 Objective**
Master the ability to evaluate claims, detect fallacies, and assess the strength of evidence.

**1.2 The Skeptic Mindset**
Believe nothing without evidence. Question the source, the motive, and the data.

**1.3 The Core Audit**
Identify Claim -> Identify Evidence -> Evaluate Logic -> Assess Conclusion.

**1.4 Tools for Analysis**
Combines `lateral-thinking`, `literature-review`, and deep logical reasoning.

**1.5 Pro-Tip**
Use this skill to audit "AI Hallucinations" or biased news articles before acting on them.

---

### Chapter 2: Identifying Logical Fallacies
**2.1 Objective**
Detect common errors in reasoning that invalidate an argument.

**2.2 Ad Hominem & Straw Man**
Attacking the person instead of the argument, or misrepresenting an opponent's view.

**2.3 Slippery Slope & False Dilemma**
Claiming a small step will lead to disaster, or pretending there are only two options.

**2.4 Confirmation Bias**
Searching only for data that confirms what you already believe.

**2.5 Expected Outcome**
An "Error Report" identifying exactly where an argument's logic breaks down.

---

### Chapter 3: Evaluating Evidence Strength
**3.1 Objective**
Distinguish between anecdotes, correlation, and proven causation.

**3.2 The Hierarchy of Evidence**
(Weak) Anecdote -> Case Study -> Cohort Study -> Randomized Control Trial -> Systematic Review (Strong).

**3.3 Sample Size & Margin of Error**
Is the data set large enough to be statistically significant?

**3.4 Funding & Bias**
Who paid for the study? Does the author have a financial interest in the outcome?

**3.5 Pro-Tip**
Ask the agent: "What is the *weakest* piece of evidence in this entire document?"

---

### Chapter 4: Assessing Source Credibility
**4.1 Objective**
Determine if a source is reliable, expert, and objective.

**4.2 Expertise Audit**
Does the author have relevant training or a track record in this specific field?

**4.3 Publication Venue**
Was it published in a peer-reviewed journal or a personal blog?

**4.4 Track Record**
Has this source been consistently accurate or consistently biased in the past?

**4.5 Expected Outcome**
A "Trust Score" for the information based on the quality of its origin.

---

### Chapter 5: Deconstructing Complex Claims
**5.1 Objective**
Break down "Big Ideas" into smaller, testable sub-claims.

**5.2 Identifying Implicit Assumptions**
What is being assumed as "True" without being stated?

**5.3 The "Necessary vs. Sufficient" Test**
Is a factor required for the outcome, or is it the *only* thing needed?

**5.4 Causal Mapping**
Draw a logical path from the premise to the conclusion. Does it ever skip a step?

**5.5 Pro-Tip**
Use this on your own `plan.md` files to find hidden architectural risks.

---

### Chapter 6: Analyzing Statistical Data
**6.1 Objective**
Detect "Lying with Statistics" and misinterpretations of data.

**6.2 P-Hacking & Selective Reporting**
Selecting only the data that shows a significant result while hiding the rest.

**6.3 Correlation vs. Causation**
Just because A and B happened together doesn't mean A caused B.

**6.4 Absolute vs. Relative Risk**
Understand how numbers are presented to sound more or less extreme.

**6.5 Expected Outcome**
A "Hard Data" audit that exposes the mathematical reality behind the marketing speak.

---

### Chapter 7: Synthesizing the Critique
**7.1 Objective**
Write a balanced, objective evaluation that highlights both strengths and flaws.

**7.2 The "Steel Man" Argument**
Argue the opponent's case *better* than they did before you tear it down.

**7.3 Weighting the Evidence**
Which arguments are central to the conclusion, and which are just "noise"?

**7.4 Final Verdict**
State clearly if the conclusion is "Proven," "Plausible," or "Unfounded."

**7.5 Pro-Tip**
Use this to review a competitor's product claims and find the "Truth" behind the hype.

---

### Chapter 8: Reference & Checklist
**8.1 Objective**
Quick lookup for critical analysis triggers.

**8.2 The "Red Flag" List**
Vague language, appeal to emotion, missing data, and circular reasoning.

**8.3 FAQ: Is this for politics?**
No. It is for Science, Engineering, Business, and any field where truth matters.

**8.4 FAQ: Can I use it on myself?**
Yes. In fact, you *should*. Use it to audit your own beliefs and technical decisions.

**8.5 Final Pro-Tip**
Critical analysis isn't about being "negative." It's about being "right."
